1. Introduction¶

Mutual funds have emerged as one of the most important investment instruments for institutional investors and private households. These funds invest and manage a significant part of the capital of insurance companies, pension funds, but also private wealth management. According to the Investment Company Fact Book, regulated funds managed \$60.1 trillion in net assets globally at the end of 2022, while \$27 trillion was invested in equity funds. Better investment decisions through an enhanced fund selection process can play a crucial role in improving the financial health of insurers and pension funds, which are under pressure due to demographic changes in an ageing society. However, with around 46'000 equity funds available globally competing for capital, a systematic fund selection process can be difficult and it can be questioned whether it guarantees any advantage.

Weather predicting mutual fund performance is feasable has been tackled by researchers for decades. In a seminal work, Carhart (1997) found that after controlling for the market return, size, book-to-market and momentrum factors (the Carhart-four-factor model), mutual fund performance is not persistent beyond a one-year horizon. Yet, empirically, we observe capital inflows in funds with high past performance and outflows from underperforming funds. Berk and Green (2004) propose a theoretical model that reconciles this behaviour with the results from Carhart (1997): Rational investors allocate capital to superior performing funds and compete with eachother in identifying heterogenous managerial skills. However, with this, investors diminish the expected outperformance of funds as Berk and Green assume decreasing returns with increasing scale of operations. This can (partly) be explained by higher costs for better performing funds (see Berk and Van Binsbergen (2015)) and higher price impact and execution costs for larger actively managed portfolios (Berk and Green (2004)), leaving a net alpha close to zero for investors.

The fact that mutual fund performance is not persistent does not imply that actively managed portfolios cannot outpeform. In fact, results regarding the benefits of investing in actively managed funds is wiedely discussed: Kacperczyk, Nieuwerburgh, and Veldkamp (2014) (and subsequently Kacperczyk, Van Nieuwerburgh, and Veldkamp (2016)) find evidence that a fraction of fund managers realize excess returns while Berk and Van Binsbergen (2015) and Fama and French (2010) find little evidence that a small fraction of fund managers generate positive alphas after costs.

If we indeed assume that positive net alpha can be realized by the most successful funds, how can we identify them if research tells us that persistency in mutual fund performance does not exist? While classical econometric methods did not allow for forecasting future fund perromance, the advent of machine learning models in the last decade equipped researches with new tools to tackle the question if mutual fund performance can be anticipated.

1.1 Machine Learning and Mutual Fund Selection¶

Gu, Kelly, and Xiu (2020) use the following arguments on why machine learning should be considered for the prediction of fund performance:

  1. Many machine learning models are powerful with a large set of predictors which more traditional methods could not handle well.
  2. A bigger set of predictors increases the risk of multicolinearity. Yet, some machine learning methods exhibit regularization techniques which can handle such highly correlated datasets well.
  3. Traditional econometrics and asset pricing require a thorough understanding of the functional form of the used models. It is difficult to asses if different factors relate to eachother linearly or if even non-linearities are present. Furhtermore, it is often unclear which interactions of variables should enter the model and where. Machine learning models can well handle non-linearities and models such as neural networks neural networks are powerful universal "approximators" of underlying relationships which is why it is not required to assume an explicit functional form of the model.

In the last few years, several researchers studied the use of machine learning for fund selection and publisehd promising results. In the following, I review some important research articles. The list is of mentioned articles is not exhaustive for the area of machine learning applications in empirical asset pricing and mutual fund research but I consider the following works to be most relevant for the subsequent study.

Kaniel et al. (2022) conclude that machine learning models can distinguish between high and low-performing mutual funds. Furthermore, they find that this differentiation is possible by using fund charactersitics only while information on stock holdings is not predictive for future performance. In a similar work, DeMiguel et al. (2021) also find that machine learning applied to fund characteristics helps to construct efficient long-only portfolios with economically large positive alphas net of costs. Wu et al. (2021) also apply machine learning models in the cross-section for fund selection. However, as opposed to the afformentioned works, they focus on hedge funds instead of open-end mutual equity funds. Nevertheless, they conclude that cross-sectional forecast based on fund specific information outperforms forecasts using time series regression.

Li and Rossi (2020) focus on mutual funds' hodlings and stock characteristics (exposure to factors) and find that especially boosted regression trees are a powerful tool for detecting significant performance predictability, yet, these predictability is time varying. Chu et al. (2022) focus on deep learning models and apply these in a time series framework as opposed to the cross-sectional approaches used in the afformentioned articles. They conlcude that deep learning models that exhibit $memory$ are superior in forecast accuracy than more traditonal statistical methods.

2.2 Target Variables¶

How do we evaluate the perfromance of a mutual fund? A first approach would be simply going for the funds that realized the highest excess returns and therefore seem to create most value for investors. This type of return prediction was used by Wu et al. (2021), yet, one could argue that this type of target might be flawed as higher risk usually implies higher returns and, thus, simply more risky mutual funds will be selected eventually. To overcome ths shortfall, DeMiguel et al. (2021) and Kaniel et al. (2022) use realized alpha as the target variable (called abnormal return in Kaniel et al. (2022)). The realized alpha is the remainder of a return which cannot be pinned down to exposure to risk factors commonly used in standard finance factor models. Beacause of this, (positive) alpha is commonly used as a proxy for an asset manager's skills. The following argumentation for the alpha as a target variable appears in Kaniel et al. (2022):

”The level of fund returns is extremely hard to predict, while the relative performance is more predictable. Abnormal returns remove the level effect of market and other risk factors. Hence, an abnormal return prediction objective is mainly a relative objective. (...) In summary, abnormal returns might be a better objective for machine learning prediction in general, and in particular for our question.”

Speaking of relative performance measure, Chu et al. (2022) use the Sharpe ratio as a target variable in their work. The Sharpe ratio scales the excess return by the standard deviation of the realized returns and is therefore a ratio of the reward to variability. If time allows, I will look into this performance measure as it is widely accepted and used in the asset management industry and its simplicity of interpretation is appealing.

Unfortunately, alpha and Sharpe ratio require a history of past returns for their calculation. This essentially means that alpha and Sharpe ratio are to some extend smoothed measures where some returns have already appeared in training (which might might be the reason that the afformentioned quote talks about better predictablity in such measures). One goal of this study is to assess if relative measures are indeed better suited for fund selection or if we also find predictability returns.

The calculation of the realized alpha invovles the following steps:

  1. Estimate the factor coefficients using formula (1) above for the previous 36 months ($t-36$ to $t-1$).
  2. Then, I use these estimated coefficients to infer at time $t$ the estimated excess return using the new factor returns $F_t$.
  3. This estimated return is subtracted from the actual excess return realized at $t$ to calcualte the realized alpha (abnormal return): $$ \alpha_{i,t}=r_{i,t}-F_t\hat{\beta}_{i,t-1}$$ where $i$ denotes to fund $i$ and $F_t$ are the (weighted) factor returns.

3. Methodology¶

3.1 Data Preparation¶

Machine learning models that are optimized numerically using gradient descent such as neural networks can exhibit model instability when working with unprocessed data. To ensure a more stable behaviour in the optimization steps, researches scale the input variables of the data on a similar interval and treate outliers.3

I follow the approach in Coqueret and Guida (2020) and preprocess the explanatory variables for every date (for each month) seperately. The preprocessing steps involve:

  • Winsorizing the data on the 0.5% and 99.5% perenctiles as I cannot exlucde the possiblity of erroneous data in MS.
  • Scaling the data.

The reason to scale the data for all funds within a specific date individually are outlined in Coqueret and Guida (2020) but the main issues when neglecting this step in my panel data setup are the following:

  • First, scaling and winsorizing data across multiple dates can lead to a spill-over of future information into training data. Basically, earlier data is scaled and winsorized based on future realizations which in practice would not have been available at that time.
  • Second, assume variables with a time trend in the data. For example, in my data, there is the column Total Net Assets which can grow over time. Preprocessing the data across multiple dates would lead to a smaller scaled value for ealier months of the fund and larger values once it grew over time. This information, howerver, is not very useful. In this study, I try to capture predictability in the true cross-section of data which means that scaling should be only applied for one point in time such that larger firms at point $t$ have larger scaled values and smaller firms smaller scaled values. Scaling across multiple time periods would disturbe this cross-sectional prespective and in general, data points in earlier time periods would tend to exhibit smaller scaled values.

After having tried out many different scaling methods, I have opted for the Robust Scaler implementation from the machine learning python library Scikit learn. While many machine learning applications use normal standardization such that the data has mean 0 and standard deviation of 1, the Robust Scaler is more robust to outliers in the data. As I am dealing with a very heterogenous data sample and cannot rule out the presenc of erronous data, the robust scaler seems to be a more suitable and conservative scaling method. As opposed to working with the mean and standard deviation which are influened by extreme data points, the robust scaler works works by subtracting the median from the data point and dividing it by the interquartil range (IQR) which is the difference between the 75th and 25th percentile of the data:

$$x_{i,scaled}=\frac{x_i-median(x)}{IQR(x)}$$

Other scaling methods I considered were:

  • Standardization
  • Min-Max Scaling
  • Uniformization
  • Hyperbolic Tangent

The robust scaler exhibits the best model stablity for the neural network and seems efficient in dealing with extreme realizations, therefore I have chosen this scaling method. Note that I have used scaled data as an input for all machine learning models, even if it is theoretically not required for tree-based models.

It is also common to have missing data points for certain funds in certain months. Fortunately, the features I work with are mostly return-related which are usually well reported in MS. In case of missing data, I apply Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE). In general, this technique fills in missing values by trying to estimate a missing value y using some are all other columns X in the data. I am using the implementation of the python package Miceforest which fits Gradient Boosting Trees to estimate missing values. It may seem to be a bit of a stretch to already apply machine learning in the preprocessing step, howerver, many studies report the benefit and robustness of MICE (e.g., see Shah et al. (2014)) especially in combination of tree models which can handle nonlinear relationships in the data. I argue in favour of applying MICE as I am not fully convinced with the idea of filling in the median or mean for missing data in my heterogenous data. I would also be careful with using the vlaue of 0 for missing data (as done in DeMiguel et al. (2021)) as this can have a strong financial interpretation. Furthermore, using imputation allows to keep more data in my analysis as I do not discard rows with missing values.

Lastly, a word of caution: Imputation can quickly lead to a spill-over of future information to ealier data. Therefore, I only impute the data seperately within each training set instead of all available data. I never used imputation on target variables but I discarded months in which there were no reported returns (rarely the case). However, I wisorized the the target variables as some returns seemed very doubtful when exploring the data.

3: For other types of data and machine learning tasks, it is also common to scale the target variables, however, in my procedure where I scale data for each date separately, the interpretation of scaled predicted variables is not straigtforward anymore and the inverse transformation to the original scale is not uniquely specified. A scaled value of 0.8 can represent a different realized excess return in the month of May 2021 as in October 2021 as the scaling only happens relatively within a cross-section. Moreover, Coqueret and Guida (2020) who work with cross-sectional data write about the importance of scaling for the input variables but do not mention the role of scaling for the target in the context of financial predicitons. Unfrotunatnely, the procedure in the other related literature is not reported.↩

3.2 Prediction Framework¶

How much do we learn from history? It is not straighforward to answer this in the context of finance and noisy data. An important early work which looked into the choice of months for portfolio formation and holding period was written by Jegadeesh and Titman (1993) who found that the most profitable momentum-based equity strategy works by looking at the returns form the previous 12 months and holding the portfolio for 3 months. Other works, such as Novy-Marx (2012) question the ipmortance of the previous 6 months prior to portfolio formation and Goyal and Wahal (2015) even fail to replicate the 'echo' effect from past returns to future returns outside the US.

It is easy to see that many different portfolios with differnt holding periods could be formed that also differ in the amount of history in which they look into the past. For computational reasons, I do not change the length of the training set (the past information) but I try out different holding periods to see if predictability can be achieved while looking further into the future.

For each portfolio formed, I only consider 12 months of past data. While this seems to go against the common paradigm that more data is always better in machine learning, I am doubtful whether this statement also holds for financial data. Is information from the early 1980s still relevant for portfolios in 2022 or later? In an earlier study, I tried out different window sizes for the training set and found that the best portfolios were formed by only looking at 12 months of past data while the portfolio performance worsened with larger training sets (going fruther back into the past). Consdidering also training time, computational resrources and energy consumption, it is also more convenient to work with only 12 months of training data.

In line with the notation in Coqueret and Guida (2020), I use the following notation:

  • $\Delta_h$ represents the holding period of the portfolio (= rebalancing frequency). I evalute the different holding periods: 3, 6, 12, 24 and 36 months.
  • $\Delta_l$ represents the horizon over which the target is computed, e.g., 3 month cumulative returns oder 6 months. In my case, the holding period of the portfolio and the horizon of target variables is the same. That is, I use 3 month cumulative excess returns for a portfolio which is then hold for 3 months, however, one could use differnt approaches and train on 3 month returns but hold the portfolio for a different amount. Thus, in my case: $\Delta_h=\Delta_l$
  • $\Delta_s$ represents the horizon of the desired training sample. In my case, this is 12 months.

The danger zone in red highlights the importance of stopping the training sample at $\Delta_l$ months ealier than $t$ when the portfolio is formed. Assume I train a predictive model for 3 months of cumulative excess returns and hold a portfolio for this period. The portfolio is formed and entered on the 1st of January. Then, $\Delta_h$ represents the months of October, November and December of the ealier year. If I would include this danger zone in the training, then for the month of October, I would include target variables that glimpse into the month of Janaury of the following year and, thus, would introduce future information which is not available in practice. This higlights why actually $\Delta_l+\Delta_s$ data are required while only $\Delta_s$ is used for the actual machine learning model training. For my data, this potential danger can only come from the targer variable y as I have constructed all features X such that they are backwards looking, i.e, the 12 month Sharpe ratio for a specific fund for the month of July takes into account the past 12 months and does not represent the Sharpe ratio of the next 12 months after July.

Working with the 12 months of training prior to portfolio formation ($\Delta_s$) implies that I work with a rolling window aproach as opposed to an extending window which would grow the training sample over time. Yet, I use the years of 1980 until 1999 as a buffer period which is used to traing the models for the first portfolio construction at the beginning of 2000 as these earlier years exhibit less data per year as later years.

3.3 Machine Learning Model Choice¶

The choice of machine learning models was based on the related literature and what was considered sensible with regards to training time and computational resources. The following models are tested for predictive power:

  • Elastic Net: This is the only linear model I consider. Regularization which is used in lasso and ridge regression is combined to prevent overfitting and focus on the most predictive features.
  • Gradient Boosting: Gradient boosting still dominates in many machine learning competions on kaggle. It is a tree based model which proved to be powerful for financial forecasting according to related literature.
  • Neural Network: I test a feedforwad neural network for predictive power. Neural networks prove to be powerful in domains outside of finance (speech, text, image classification etc.).
